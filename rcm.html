Product approach

Design an Agentic AI platform for revenue cycle management.

Start with operating systems, not with a general chatbot for billing teams. Break the problem into a small number of systems: how work enters the platform, how decisions are made, how actions are executed, and where human review must stay in the loop.

This page is written in a PM style. It explains the problem framing, the system boundaries, the agent model, the standards that constrain the design, and the phased rollout needed to make the product credible in a real provider environment.

Product Thesis

Build a governed revenue operating system, not a loose collection of AI demos.

Use a simple operating principle: API and EDI first, operator automation second, human oversight always visible. If the platform cannot explain why it acted, what rule it used, and who approved the outcome, it is not ready for enterprise RCM.

Problem Decomposition

Define the product problem in four layers before designing agents.

This keeps the roadmap grounded. It prevents the product from becoming a vague promise of automation without a clear understanding of where value is created and where risk is introduced.

Layer 1 Business outcome

Where does the money leak?

The business problem is not "billing is manual." The business problem is that cash leaks at predictable points: eligibility issues, missing authorization, weak documentation, coding variance, claim rejections, underpayments, and delayed appeal handling.

Layer 2 Operating workflow

Which team owns the issue today?

Each leakage point maps to an operating team: front office, coding, billing, denial management, or payer follow-up. The product needs to improve those workflows before it tries to replace them.

Layer 3 Decision system

What drives the decision?

The platform must know whether the decision is driven by policy text, payer behavior, contract terms, coding rules, prior history, or a hard compliance boundary. That distinction determines which part should be model-based and which part must stay deterministic.

Layer 4 Intervention model

What should the product actually do?

Once the source of value and risk is clear, the product can choose the right intervention: surface insight, create a task, draft the next action, execute a bounded action, or escalate to a reviewer. That is the real product design decision.

Market Read

The useful lesson from products like Youlify and AiRCM is not their copywriting. It is what they reveal about system priorities.

I read the market as a signal of what matters operationally: centralized work management, human-supervised automation, payer visibility, and deep integration with existing systems.

Youlify official site

Signal: command center plus AI plus experts

Youlify emphasizes a command center, AI agents, bulk action workflows, and expert oversight. That tells me the product needs a work orchestration layer, not just a user-facing assistant.

  • Product implication: one place to prioritize work across multiple RCM domains.
  • Architecture implication: one shared claim timeline, queue model, and evidence trail.
  • Design response: make the command center the operating core, not only a reporting surface.
AiRCM NexxGen AIRCM

Signal: integrations, rules, remit handling, denial replay

AiRCM-style products usually stress bidirectional EHR integration, remit automation, denial replay, and rules engines. That tells me the moat is in the system of action, not only in the user experience.

  • Product implication: teams want action, not just recommendations.
  • Architecture implication: the platform needs a canonical claim graph, not prompt-only context assembly.
  • Design response: treat policies, contracts, remits, and denials as versioned operational assets.
Conclusion Product stance

The hard part is control and accountability

The market already shows there is demand for AI in RCM. The real challenge is deciding when the platform may act, what evidence it must show, which rules it must honor, and how a manager can audit or reverse the outcome.

  • Use LLMs for interpretation, extraction, reasoning, and drafting.
  • Use deterministic workflow for actions, permissions, thresholds, and review routing.
  • Make expert oversight a designed product tier, not a fallback after failure.

Architecture

Build the product as a layered operating model, not as one large autonomous agent.

This keeps the system legible. It also lets the business adopt more automation over time without losing control of approval paths, evidence requirements, or audit history.

Layer 01

Work Surfaces

Different users need different views of the same operating truth: billers, coders, denial specialists, RCM managers, finance leaders, and outsourced service teams.

Operations cockpit Coder review Denial desk Manager dashboard
Layer 02

Command Center and Workflow Orchestration

Put a single orchestration layer in the middle of the product. It owns prioritization, task decomposition, SLA routing, escalation, and reviewer assignment across all RCM functions.

Layer 03

Specialist Agent Runtime

Each agent should have a narrow charter, a fixed tool set, and a structured output. No agent should own the full claim lifecycle by itself.

This layer combines retrieval, policy parsing, contract normalization, rules, variance detection, and document intelligence. It is the reasoning infrastructure behind the agents.

Operating principles

Keep these operating rules explicit from day one.

  • Tool permissions: each agent gets the minimum action surface it needs.
  • Evidence contracts: recommendations must carry source, version, effective date, and confidence.
  • Human gates: coding release, major appeals, and sensitive collections stay approval-based.
  • Replayability: every decision should be traceable by claim, payer, agent, and model version.
  • Evaluation: release through shadow mode, canary mode, and bounded automation.
  • PHI hygiene: retrieval scope, retention, and redaction should be configurable.

Execution Playbooks

Prove the platform with three closed-loop workflows before promising end-to-end autonomy.

Playbook 01 · Pre-Service Collection and Authorization
  1. An appointment event enters the platform with patient, coverage, procedure, and site-of-service context.
  2. The pre-service agent runs eligibility and benefit checks using 270 or 271 and payer APIs where available.
  3. If authorization is needed, the system checks policy requirements and prior authorization rules.
  4. If supporting information is missing, the product creates a checklist task for staff instead of free-form model behavior.
  5. The system estimates patient responsibility and recommends the right collection path.
  6. Sensitive balances and special financial cases move to a human queue with the evidence already assembled.
Playbook 02 · Documentation, Coding, Claim Creation, and Scrub
  1. When the note is signed, document intelligence extracts diagnoses, procedure candidates, and missing evidence.
  2. The coding agent compares the extracted facts against code sets, edits, specialty playbooks, and payer rules.
  3. The output is structured: code suggestions, rationale, and a list of documentation tasks.
  4. The claim builder creates a draft claim and runs payer-specific rule packs before clearinghouse submission.
  5. Complex cases move into governed coding review before release.
  6. After submission, 277CA and downstream status are tracked so rejections feed prevention logic upstream.
Playbook 03 · Denial Recovery and Payer Surveillance
  1. When the 835 arrives, the system parses remittance codes, payment variance, and patient responsibility.
  2. The denial agent clusters root cause while the surveillance agent checks whether the issue reflects broader payer drift.
  3. Single-claim issues become correction or appeal tasks. Payer-wide issues become bulk action opportunities.
  4. The policy agent checks recent policy or guide changes that may explain the variance.
  5. Appeals, rebills, and underpayment recovery pass through release gates before execution.
  6. The resolution outcome updates prevention logic across coding, documentation, and front-end workflow.

Standards and Constraints

Design the product around industry constraints from the start.

These are not reference notes. They are design inputs that determine the data model, integration model, release process, and credibility of the product in the market.

X12 and HIPAA Transactions

Eligibility, claim status, prior authorization, claims, and remittance workflows must align with the standard transaction model. The product should treat those transactions as first-class operating objects, not imported files.

Design implication: every workflow needs a transaction view, an acknowledgement path, and an exception path.

CAQH CORE and Payer Variance

Supporting the transaction standard is not enough. Payers still vary through operating rules and companion guides. The platform needs an adapter model that can preserve those differences cleanly.

Design implication: standard transactions and payer-specific rules need to be versioned together.

FHIR and Prior Authorization APIs

The CMS interoperability and prior authorization direction means the product cannot stop at X12. As of March 13, 2026, parts of the operational requirements are already in effect and the broader API requirements are approaching the 2027 deadline.

Design implication: one canonical model should support both transaction and API-based execution.

Code Sets and Effective Dates

ICD-10, HCPCS, NCCI, LCD, and NCD rules all change over time. Recommendations must be date-aware or they become brittle the moment a rule set changes.

Design implication: every decision should store service date, policy date, and rule version.

HIPAA Security and Audit Controls

Permissions, unique identity, auditability, integrity, and transmission security are not infrastructure details. They determine whether an AI-assisted action can be trusted and reviewed later.

Design implication: audit is a visible product feature, not a hidden back-office concern.

Licensed Content Boundaries

Some code systems and mappings live behind licensing boundaries. The knowledge layer needs to respect that reality instead of treating all rule sources as generic training data.

Design implication: build a license-aware knowledge plane with entitlement checks.

Delivery Roadmap

Ship the platform in three phases.

Large provider organizations usually accept AI through a progression: better visibility first, then assisted execution, then bounded automation. The roadmap should follow that reality.

Phase 1

Visibility and Structured Assist

Start with the command center, claim timeline, policy graph, denial clustering, and payer surveillance. The first goal is better operational visibility and faster root-cause analysis.

  • Core deliverables: queue model, policy ingestion, claim graph, surveillance views.
  • Primary outcome: make the work legible and measurable.
Phase 2

Guarded Workflow Execution

Add approval gates, coding assist, pre-service collection workflows, low-risk rebill paths, and touchless posting where reconciliation is clean.

  • Core deliverables: bulk actions, reviewer routing, exception queues, rule-bound execution.
  • Primary outcome: measurable productivity and cleaner downstream operations.
Phase 3

Bounded Automation and Optimization

Only after the control plane is mature would I allow bounded auto mode for defined low-risk workflows, with continuous evaluation and clear rollback paths.

  • Core deliverables: automation policies, adaptive payer playbooks, online evaluation.
  • Primary outcome: higher throughput without losing governance.
